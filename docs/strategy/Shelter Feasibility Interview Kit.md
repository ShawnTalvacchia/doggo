# Shelter Feasibility Interview Kit

> **What this is.** The questions the demo *can't* ask — driven by us, in conversation, NOT baked into the product. The guided shelter walkthrough is deliberately question-light (its soft-close keeps three light questions); the full feasibility set lives here. Built in Phase 2 "The Shelter's Side" alongside the operator surfaces it interrogates.
>
> **Governing principle: desirability before feasibility.** The demo paints an appealing, clear picture of the shelter's side first (the operator view — handover board, application queue, walker pool, adoption-interest landing). Only *then*, in conversation, do we ask the hard questions: the binding constraint, liability, whether latent demand converts. Lead with the demo; reach for this kit after it's landed.
>
> **Companion docs.** The beat-by-beat demo + its assumption callouts live in [[strategy/mentor-network-shelter-demo]] (the A1–A10 crosswalk). The strategic frame — the four axes (visibility / accountability / competence / control) and the first-conversation questions — lives in [[strategy/Doggo_Positioning_and_Three_Worlds]] (§3 + Appendix). This kit operationalizes both into an interview runbook.

---

## How to run an interview

1. **Show the demo first.** Walk the shelter operator view (`?as` the shelter-operator persona, or `?admin=1`): the handover board, the application queue, the walker pool, the adoption-interest landing. Let them react. Desirability before feasibility.
2. **Then open the ladder** (Part 1) — the single most important sequence. It tells us whether World 3 converts at all.
3. **Rank the pain-point map** (Part 2) with them — concrete, comparative, fast.
4. **Walk the per-surface prompts** (Part 3) for the surfaces they reacted to — "here's how we'd handle X; does this fit how you actually do it?"
5. **Close on the hard feasibility** (Part 4) — liability, insurance, the binding constraint.
6. **After the interview**, edit the affected assumption entries in [[strategy/mentor-network-shelter-demo]] and [[planning/Open Questions & Assumptions Log]] **in place** — don't append a second contradictory note.

## Part 1 — The pain-point ladder *(the load-bearing sequence)*

This **replaces** the old applicants-vs-vetting-cost binary in Open Questions §14. That binary forced a single fork; the ladder lets the shelter tell us where the constraint actually sits, and whether solving it changes anything.

Ask in order:

1. **What limits how many of your dogs get out and get adopted today?** *(Open. Let them name it before we suggest anything.)*
2. **Walk us through what's actually painful about taking on and running volunteer walkers.** *(Then have them rank the pain-point map, Part 2.)*
3. **If your top one or two pains there were solved, would you take on more walkers — or shorter commitments? And would that move the answer to question 1?** *(The conversion test: does easing volunteer pain actually move getting-dogs-out-and-adopted, or is the constraint elsewhere — kennel space, staff hours at the handoff, adopter demand?)*
4. **Would you accept a trusted volunteer's vouch in place of your own intake assessment?** *(The mentor-vouch substitution — A1, the single load-bearing assumption. If "no," the mentor session becomes a credibility booster, not a substitute; that fallback is already built.)*

**Why the ladder matters:** it separates three different worlds the binary collapsed —
- constraint is **vetting/coordination cost** → Doggo absorbs it, rules can loosen (the optimistic case);
- constraint is **staff handoff** → a hard limit we respect; we ease everything *around* the handoff but never automate it;
- constraint is **downstream** (kennel space, adopter demand, staff capacity) → more walkers doesn't move the needle, and we should know that before building further.

## Part 2 — The volunteer pain-point map *(rank these with them)*

Hand them these nine and ask them to rank — or just to point at the top two or three. Comparative ranking surfaces more than "is X a problem? (yes)."

| Pain | One-line gloss | Which operator surface speaks to it |
|---|---|---|
| **Vetting / onboarding staff time** | The hours it takes to assess and orient each new walker | Application queue + mentor-vouch path |
| **Trust & liability** | Fear of handing a dog to someone who turns out wrong | Vouching, waivers, ID, the four axes |
| **Skill mismatch** | The walker can't handle the dog they took | Per-dog/tier eligibility gating; mentored first walk |
| **Reliability / churn** | Volunteers flake or drift off after a few weeks | Walker pool history; the standing group walk as retention |
| **Coordination overhead** | The email/WhatsApp scheduling grind | The handover board (today's walks at a glance) |
| **Documentation / accountability** | No record of who had which dog when | Logged check-out → back-safe check-in trail |
| **Supervision capacity** | Not enough staff to oversee walkers | Mentor-as-supervisor; group walk (one mentor, N dogs) |
| **Volunteer motivation / impact** | Keeping volunteers feeling useful and seen | Walk recaps → advocacy loop → adoptions |
| **Physical checkout / release** | The literal handoff of N dogs, esp. for a group | The handover board's group multi-dog release proposal |

**Note what's NOT on the demo's critical path.** If they rank *kennel space* or *adopter demand* above everything here, that's the Part 1 question 3 signal: easing volunteer pain won't move their #1, and we should hear that loudly.

## Part 3 — Per-surface discussion prompts

For each operator surface they reacted to, the frame is the same: **"here's how we'd handle X — does this fit how you actually do it?"** These are design-validation prompts, not yes/no.

### Handover board ("today's walks")
- This shows every dog due out, out now, and back safe on one screen. Is "at a glance, who has which dog right now" something you lack today — or do you already have it (a whiteboard, a sheet)?
- Would a board like this *replace* a step you do now, or be one more thing to keep updated?

### Check-out → back-safe check-in (the logged trail)
- We log when a dog is released and when it's confirmed back safe — a custody trail. Is that level of record useful to you, overkill, or exactly what an incident would need after the fact?
- Who, physically, would tap "back safe" — the same staffer who released the dog, the walker, a kennel tech?

### Group walk — multi-dog release *(the open FC18 question)*
- We're proposing the mentor signs out the whole group as the responsible party — one check-out for N dogs, not one per dog. **Does that match how you'd hand off several dogs at once, or is that a non-starter?**
- Is releasing several dogs to one outing operationally heavier for you than one-at-a-time — and is the mentor-as-responsible-party the right way to carry that?

### Application queue
- Applications land here with the person's note and (if they've done mentor sessions) a recommendation line. Invite → vouch. Is this roughly your intake, or are there steps in between we're skipping (a call, a reference check, a trial)?

### Walker pool
- Walkers carry an earned tier from walk count, but you can promote or demote anyone, any time — your call always wins over the math. Is that the right balance, or would you want tiers to be entirely your call from the start?

### Adoption-interest landing
- Interest mostly comes from someone seeing a walk recap, not from the adopter having walked the dog themselves. Does that match what you see — that exposure, not the walker becoming the adopter, is what drives adoptions?
- When someone's interested, you arrange the meet-and-greet (no auto-match). Is shelter-curated the right model, or would you want the platform to do more matching?

## Part 4 — The hard feasibility *(close here)*

The demo eases *comfort*; what eases *liability* is insurance and a waiver a lawyer signs off on. These come last, in conversation (from [[strategy/Doggo_Positioning_and_Three_Worlds]] Appendix):

1. When you say weekdays 9–5, is that because that's when staff can hand off a dog, or because vetting and coordinating volunteers is too much work? *(The binding constraint — ties back to Part 1.)*
2. If walkers arrived pre-vetted, ID-verified, and pre-scheduled, could you accept more of them — or shorter commitments — even with every handoff still during staff hours?
3. What does your insurance currently cover for volunteer walks, and what would it require to expand?
4. Has an incident ever happened? What did you wish you'd had when it did?
5. Would a day-trip or an overnight (picked up during staff hours) be easier or harder for you than more frequent short walks?
6. Would your lawyer accept a platform baseline waiver + your own layer on top, or must everything be your paper? *(A2.)*
