# Doggo — Business Strategy (Draft)

Working document. These are ideas for discussion — not a final plan. Revenue models, development paths, and growth angles to explore.

---

## Revenue Ideas

### 1. Care transaction fee (10–15%)

The core revenue stream. When a connected user books care through the platform, Doggo takes a percentage. The community builds the trust; the platform earns when that trust converts to paid care.

**Why users would pay through the platform instead of Venmo/cash:**
- Clear record — dates, price, pet details, confirmed in writing
- Cancellation and change handling — structured beats awkward texts
- Care history — "Petra has watched 4 dogs from this group with no issues"
- Payment without the awkward — paying through the app normalises paying a friend
- Insurance/liability coverage (eventually)

**Risk:** Users may arrange care off-platform once they're connected. The platform needs to offer enough value (records, protection, convenience) to justify the fee.

### 2. Business profiles (freemium directory)

Local pet businesses — groomers, vets, pet shops — get community-embedded listings. Not ads. Part of the neighbourhood.

- Free tier: basic listing, location, contact
- Paid tier: enhanced visibility, reviews, community endorsements, promoted in relevant group contexts

**Why it works:** Businesses already want to reach engaged local dog owners. Doggo's audience is hyper-targeted and trust-oriented — better than generic advertising.

### 3. Premium features (later, careful)

Advanced group tools, expanded gallery storage, priority visibility for providers. Community features stay free — no paywalls on core social functionality.

**Principles:**
- Never paywall meets, connections, or messaging
- Premium = power-user tools, not basic access
- If it feels like extortion, don't ship it

### 4. Sponsored meets & events

Local businesses sponsor community events — a pet shop sponsors a weekend walk, a vet sponsors a puppy socialisation meet. Brand gets visibility; community gets better events.

**Why it works:** Low-friction for sponsors. Authentic — the event exists regardless, the sponsor just makes it better. No banner ads.

### 5. Data insights (anonymised, opt-in)

Neighbourhood-level trends — popular walking times, dog breed density, care demand patterns. Useful for pet businesses, urban planners, vets. Strictly anonymised and aggregated.

**Caution:** Only viable at significant scale. Privacy must be non-negotiable. Opt-in only.

### 6. White-label or licensing

If the community-first model proves itself in Prague, the platform model (not just the code) could be licensed to other cities or verticals. Dog owner communities have similar patterns globally.

**When:** Much later. Prague needs to work first.

## Development Paths

Three directions for how we build this. Not mutually exclusive — but each frames investment and timelines differently.

### Option A — Hand off to developers

Use the prototype as a spec. Hire a development team to build the production app. Our role shifts to product direction.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Pros** | Faster to production quality. Professional engineering. |
| **Cons** | Expensive. Requires a clear, stable spec. Risk of losing the product feel in translation. |
| **Budget** | Significant — likely €50–100k+ for MVP. |
| **Timeline** | 3–6 months to production MVP. |

### Option B — Seek investment

Use the prototype + validation data to pitch investors. Fund a proper team and launch.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Pros** | Bigger ambition. Proper resources for engineering, marketing, community seeding. |
| **Cons** | Needs strong validation data first. Equity dilution. Investor expectations shape priorities. |
| **Budget** | Investment-funded — but we need to spend on validation to get there. |
| **Timeline** | 2–4 months to validate, then fundraising cycle. |

### Option C — Keep building ourselves

Continue with AI-assisted development. Move methodically from prototype toward a real product.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Pros** | Low cost. Full control. No external pressure on priorities. |
| **Cons** | Slower. Limited by our bandwidth. Some things (payment processing, auth) will need real engineering eventually. |
| **Budget** | Minimal — time investment primarily. |
| **Timeline** | Ongoing, incremental. |

### Option D — Hybrid: validate, then decide

Invest in validation and community seeding now (Options B/C). Use data to make the A/B/C decision with evidence rather than guesses.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Pros** | No premature commitment. Data-driven decision. Lowest regret. |
| **Cons** | Delays the "real" build. Requires discipline to not over-build the prototype. |
| **Budget** | Moderate — validation budget (surveys, user tests, seeded events). |
| **Timeline** | 2–3 months of validation, then choose a path. |

## Investment Areas (Near-Term)

Regardless of development path, these investments are needed.

### Marketing plan
Budget and plan for seeding the first neighbourhoods. Targeted social media, local partnerships, physical presence at dog parks. This might matter more than the next feature.

### Community seeding
Pay early hosts to organise and run meets. Partner with local dog communities and trainers. Create density before expecting organic growth.

### Validation budget
Surveys, user tests, prototype walkthroughs. Start testing assumptions now so we're building on evidence, not guesses.

## Key Questions to Resolve

- What's our appetite for external investment vs bootstrapping?
- How much can we spend on validation and community seeding before we need revenue?
- Is Prague-only the right first market, or should we test elsewhere?
- At what validation milestone do we commit to a development path?
- What's the minimum viable community size where the product feels alive?
