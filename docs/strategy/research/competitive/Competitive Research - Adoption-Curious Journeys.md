# Competitive Research — Adoption-Curious Journeys

Research pass run 2026-06-12 (deep-research workflow, 20 sources, 25 claims adversarially verified, 22 confirmed) to inform the **Adoption-Curious Journey** phase. Question: how do shelters and adoption platforms design "explore before you commit" experiences, and what should a Prague community dog app borrow for an adoption-curious user who walks shelter dogs before deciding?

> **Evidentiary health check.** The two strongest, most product-relevant numbers (5×/14× lift; the exposure mechanism) rest on a single large 2023 observational study — correlational, not randomized, with likely selection bias (intervention dogs had longer prior stays). Several program figures come from foundation advocacy toolkits (credible sector authority, self-reported). Czech-specific evidence is thin — only a browse-then-meet confirmation, no Prague field-trip/regulatory data. Treat the *direction* as solid and the *magnitudes* as indicative.

---

## The headline reframe (this changes the phase design)

**The adoption value of walking a shelter dog is mostly NOT "the walker adopts the dog." It's that the walker becomes an ADVOCATE who surfaces the dog to *other* adopters.**

- Brief day-outings make shelter dogs **~5× more likely** to be adopted; 1–2 night sleepovers **~14×** (Maddie's Nationwide Fostering Study, Virginia Tech/ASU, *Animals* 2023, n≈27,901 dogs / 51 shelters).
- But only **~4%** of outing caregivers and **~12%** of sleepover caregivers adopt the dog themselves. "The vast majority of adopters were not the foster families." The lift comes from **community exposure** — the dog is seen, met, photographed, and shared, which surfaces it to a non-caregiver adopter (researcher Lisa Gunter).
- The two strongest *direct* "volunteers convert to adopters" claims (62% had adopted a foster; 21% fostered intending to adopt) were **refuted in verification** — so we should NOT design primarily around the walker adopting.

**Why this matters for Doggo:** our original framing ("adoption-curious person walks a dog → bonds → adopts") is real but a *minority* outcome. The *proven, higher-leverage* engine is the **advocacy loop** — and Doggo already has the parts for it: walk recaps as posts, the shelter feed, the community network, the "Adopt {dog}" CTA. A walker (adoption-curious or not) walks a shelter dog → posts photos/story → it surfaces to the network → *someone* adopts. **Design the phase for the advocacy loop first, the self-adoption path second.**

---

## Actionable patterns to build

1. **De-couple walking from adoption commitment — explicitly, in copy.** The single most-documented operational friction is people thinking "take a dog out = commit to adopt." Entry copy and flow must say *"walk a shelter dog — no adoption obligation."* This is a hard UX requirement, not a nicety. *(Maddie's Fund 2024 — "biggest challenge was helping people participate without committing to adoption.")*

2. **Walk completion → prompt photo/story capture as a first-class action.** Field-trip programs are explicitly run as *marketing* funnels ("creating marketing material is part of the job," post 2–3×/week). This IS the proven mechanism. Wire walk-finish → "Share a moment" (we already have post composition + the shelter feed). The walk recap is the adoption ad. *(Maddie's Fund toolkit; UF Shelter Medicine "Corey" exemplar — networked via a volunteer post → adopted at his first event.)*

3. **Model adoption as a state machine with non-failure exits, not a binary commit.** LifeLine's 2-week "foster-to-forever" trial has three off-ramps: finalize electronically anytime / return and try a different pet / convert to traditional fostering. Mirror this — an escalation ladder **walk → repeat walk → (sleepover/foster) → adopt** where every step has a graceful exit. *(LifeLine Animal Project.)*

4. **Returns are not failures — frame "try before commit" as the welfare-positive path.** Dog returns run ~16% within 6 months, mostly behavior/fit mismatch (behavioral 36%, pet-incompatibility 18%); the field now reframes temporary adoptions as valuable for revealing in-home behavior. The "explore before you commit" framing is on the right side of current welfare thinking. *(Powell et al. 2021, Scientific Reports, n=23,932.)*

5. **Keep the shelter as the curator of the meet-and-greet — it matches our shelter-authority principle.** The standard funnel is browse profiles → application → **org-curated** meet-and-greet → adopt; the application does NOT guarantee selection (the org picks matches). Confirmed for Czech/Prague (browse-then-meet; "nothing compares to meeting them in person"). Our shelters already hold vouching authority — adoption should route through them the same way, not auto-match. *(Cool Critters; think.cz Prague context.)*

---

## Open questions to carry into shelter interviews

- **Czech/Prague specifics are a gap.** No Prague-specific data on field-trip/day-out programs, off-site legal constraints, or adoption-funnel conversion. The only Prague source confirmed the universal browse-then-meet pattern and nothing operational. Probe this directly with coordinators.
- **Walker (not overnight foster) → adopter conversion rate is unquantified** — the surviving evidence is about *exposure to others*, not the walker's own propensity. Don't over-index on self-adoption.
- **How to instrument the advocacy mechanism without re-introducing commitment pressure** — i.e., prompt photo/share (the proven funnel) while signaling no obligation. This is the core design tension of the feature.
- **Do graceful-off-ramp trials actually reduce the ~16% return rate, and by how much?** Asserted, not quantified.
