# Doggo — Positioning & the Three Worlds

*Working draft. Written to clarify the thinking for myself; tone and depth can be adapted for Alyssa or outside readers later.*

---

## The thesis

**Better social lives for dogs — on both sides of the shelter wall.**

Doggo is a community-first platform where dog people meet through their dogs, build real trust, and turn that trust into care. People come for their own dog — walks, company, a local network, help when they need it — and in the process a community forms that also lifts the dogs who don't have homes yet. One mission, two surfaces: keep owned dogs social and well so they stay in their homes, and get shelter dogs seen and adopted.

---

## Why it matters now

Most "dog apps" sell convenience. The deeper, more durable hook is health. A dog's social life isn't a luxury — it's preventive care, and the science is unambiguous: the primary socialization window is the first three months (roughly 3–14 weeks), and under-socialized dogs develop fear, avoidance, and aggression. Those behavior problems are the number-one reason dogs are surrendered to shelters, and behavioral issues — not disease — are the leading cause of death for dogs under three.

Read that chain again, because it is the whole strategy: under-socialized → behavior problems → relinquishment and early death. "Get your dog social, early and often" is therefore one of the most protective things an owner can do — and it is the same force that keeps dogs out of shelters in the first place. That makes Doggo's community mission and its shelter mission the same story, not two separate ones:

- **Prevention** (owned dogs): social early and often → fewer behavior problems → they stay in their homes.
- **Cure** (shelter dogs): contact, outings, and advocacy → less stress and far more adoptions.

This is the wedge. We are not "a social network for dogs." We are the easiest way to give a dog the social life that keeps it healthy and homed.

---

## How it works

The engine is a simple, compounding loop: **community → trust → care.**

People join for something light and appealing — a park meetup, a neighborhood group, a trainer's free public walk. Showing up builds familiarity; familiarity becomes trust; trust is what makes someone comfortable booking, and paying for, care from a person they actually know. Reviews, repeat visits, shared meets, and credentials all accrue as visible trust signals, so the network grows more valuable the more it is used.

Two things sit on top of that loop. **Credentialing** — community-earned tiers, mentor vouching, verified identity — lets trust travel and lets the platform safely unlock higher-stakes activity like paid care and shelter walking. And a **give-back** routes a share of platform revenue to shelters, so the same activity that grows the business also funds the dogs who need it most.

## The three worlds

The platform is one place, but three kinds of people enter it for three different reasons. Each gets a world built around what they want — and each one proves something we still need to validate.

### 1. The new dog owner — *find your community*

**Who they are.** Someone who just got a dog, or moved somewhere new, and feels a little out of their depth. Anxious, well-intentioned, looking for help and belonging.

**What they come for.** Reassurance and connection: *am I doing this right, and who near me gets it?*

**The world we build for them.** A warm local feed, neighborhood and park groups, a trainer's free public walk to attend, and gentle guidance framed around the science — social early and often. Onboarding leads with their dog's wellbeing, not with a feature tour.

**What it proves.** That the on-ramp works — that a nervous newcomer lands in a community, finds trust, and the community→trust→care funnel actually turns. *(This is the path our first interviews tested most, and the signal was positive.)*

### 2. The trainer / walker — *build community, and a living*

**Who they are.** A professional, or an aspiring one, in a fragmented and largely uncredentialed local market — trainers, walkers, and sitters with no real way to signal that they are good.

**What they come for.** Clients, reputation, and income: a way to turn good work into a visible track record.

**The world we build for them.** Public walks and meets that double as lead generation; a profile where repeat clients, reviews, and credentials compound into trust; paid services including mentor walks, where they earn by supervising newer walkers toward a credential. The platform becomes the credentialing layer the market doesn't otherwise have.

**What it proves.** That the supply side is real — that a pro can build a following and earn, so there is someone on the other side of all that care demand — and that credentialing reads as valuable rather than bureaucratic.

### 3. The shelter — *get dogs adopted*

**Who they are.** An under-resourced rescue or shelter, fiercely protective of its animals, stretched thin on staff time, and wary of strangers.

**What they come for.** Adoptions, animal welfare, and help — without more work or more risk.

**The world we build for them.** Every dog has a profile and a story; vetted community members can walk them; and every walk becomes a recap that surfaces the dog to the wider network. This is the *advocacy loop*, and it is the most proven thing in the whole strategy: dogs that get out are over 5× more likely to be adopted after a day-trip outing, and over 14× after a one-to-two-night sleepover. Crucially, only about 4% of outing walkers and 12% of sleepover hosts adopt the dog themselves — the lift comes from the dog being seen, photographed, and shared to *other* adopters. Every walker becomes an advocate.

The operational reality we respect honestly: **a human will always need to release and receive the dog, during staff hours.** We do not fight that. Instead, the platform makes everything *around* the handoff faster, safer, and documented — easing the two things that actually cost a shelter (vetting and coordination) and the one thing that scares them (liability). It does this on the four axes that drive a shelter's comfort:

- **Visibility** — check-ins, GPS, photos, and a "back safe" confirmation, so they can see the dog is fine.
- **Accountability** — ID-verified walkers, waivers signed in-app, and a logged check-out / check-in trail.
- **Competence** — vouching, walk-count tiers, completed orientation, and eligibility gating so the wrong person cannot book the wrong dog.
- **Control** — the shelter can revoke a walker or flag a dog instantly; they keep the override.

The payoff is not "walks at midnight." It is that a shelter can say yes to *more* walkers and *shorter* commitments — because the expensive, scary parts are absorbed — while every handoff stays exactly as supervised as it is today. Their current rules (a real example nearby: "weekdays 9–5, one walk a week, three-month minimum") most likely exist to keep vetting and coordination manageable; lower those costs and the rules can loosen, within staff hours.

**What it proves.** That the advocacy loop plus the give-back turns a community of walkers into an adoption engine — and that a real shelter would say yes.

## The give-back, and the shape of the business

Doggo stays a for-profit — but one with a heart wired into it, not bolted on. A defined share of care revenue, plus a sponsor-a-dog surface for people who want to help but can't physically walk, routes transparently to partner shelters.

This is not charity theater; the alignment is the entire point. The research on cause marketing is clear that it can outperform ordinary promotion *when the cause genuinely fits the product* — and a dog platform funding dog shelters is about as aligned as it gets. Done in the open — show exactly where the money goes, let the donor get the credit and never the company (the Chewy model, which has moved more than $180M to shelters) — it becomes a real reason to choose us and stay, not a gimmick.

We start light: sponsor-a-dog first, graduate to a fee-share once there is revenue to share. And we keep it a give-back, not a non-profit conversion — so the upside, the speed, and the funding options all stay intact. It is the heart, the PR, and the differentiator, without giving up the business.

## Where this goes next

The plan from here is deliberately fast: finish the three demo paths so each clearly proves its one thing, validate with real users (including at least one real shelter conversation), then decide — with evidence — whether and how to build the real product.

The honest open questions we are carrying:

- **The shelter binding constraint.** Is the weekday rule about staff handoff (a hard limit we respect) or about vetting and coordination cost (which we can absorb)? Everything in World 3 hinges on this, and it is the first thing to ask.
- **Liability for real.** The features ease *comfort*; what eases *liability* is insurance and a waiver a lawyer signs off on. A demo can only gesture at that — we need the shelters' and their insurers' actual requirements.
- **Does latent weekend demand convert?** The intuition that there are people who would walk on weekends but can't commit to weekday 9–5 is strong, but unproven until a real shelter pilots it.
- **What comes after the demo.** Our own roadmap marks everything past the demo as "not yet planned." Going from a validated demo to a working product — real backend, who builds it, how the give-back is structured — is the decision waiting on the other side of validation.

None of these are reasons to slow down. They are the map of what the demos are *for*.

## Appendix — Questions for the first shelter conversation

1. When you say weekdays 9–5, is that because that's when staff can hand off a dog, or because vetting and coordinating volunteers is too much work?
2. If walkers arrived pre-vetted, ID-verified, and pre-scheduled, could you accept more of them — or shorter commitments — even with every handoff still during staff hours?
3. What is the single most time-consuming part of taking on a new volunteer walker today?
4. What would make you comfortable letting a vetted walker take a dog out — what do you need to *see* or *know*?
5. What does your insurance currently cover for volunteer walks, and what would it require to expand?
6. Has an incident ever happened? What did you wish you'd had when it did?
7. Would a day-trip or an overnight (picked up during staff hours) be easier or harder for you than more frequent short walks?
