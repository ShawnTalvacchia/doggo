# Doggo – Product Principles

These principles define the **spine of the Doggo product**. They exist to guide judgment, prevent drift, and align humans and agents around the same intent — not to describe features or UI rules.

---

## Two Versions, One Foundation

Doggo is being built as two distinct product experiences that share a common design system, signup flow, and infrastructure.

**Provider Version** — the current focus. A trust-first but marketplace-functional app for finding and booking dog care services in Prague. Ratings, reviews, and service discovery are core to this experience. Think Rover or Care.com, but built with Prague's social context in mind.

**Community Version** — parked. A proximity-based, non-commercial community layer built around familiar faces, shared routines, and social trust. No ratings, no marketplace mechanics. To be developed after the Provider Version is validated.

All shared principles below apply to both. Version-specific principles are called out separately.

---

## Shared Principles

### 1. Prague First, Global Never (for now)

Doggo is designed for Prague's specific context — cultural expectations, neighborhood density, how locals use shared spaces. Global patterns are a reference, not a requirement.

### 2. Owners Enter First

Every user starts as an owner. Provider capabilities — whether formal (Provider Version) or informal (Community Version) — are unlocked through intent and action, not assumed.

### 3. Real-World Proximity Beats Abstract Matching

Shared places, routines, and repeated encounters create stronger trust than algorithmic similarity alone. Location and physical context are first-class signals.

### 4. Low-Pressure Participation by Default

Users should be able to explore, observe, and participate without commitment. Actions should feel reversible, optional, and safe — regardless of which version they're using.

### 5. The Product Facilitates — It Does Not Force

Doggo creates opportunities for connection, trust, and help. It does not pressure users into transactions, roles, or visibility they are not ready for.

---

## Provider Version Principles

_Current development focus._

### P1. Ratings and Reviews Are Legitimate Trust Signals

Star ratings and written reviews are appropriate and expected in a service marketplace. They should be honest, earned, and displayed clearly. Inflating or suppressing them undermines trust.

### P2. Quality Over Volume

Doggo is not optimizing for the most providers or the most bookings. It is optimizing for owners finding someone genuinely good for their dog. A smaller, higher-trust provider pool beats a large, undifferentiated one.

### P3. Professional Providers Are Valid Users

Unlike the Community Version, the Provider Version explicitly supports professional or semi-professional dog care providers. Their professionalism is an asset, not a signal of inauthenticity.

### P4. Discovery Before Commitment

Owners should be able to browse, read profiles, and assess providers thoroughly before initiating any booking. Pressure tactics (urgency nudges, artificial scarcity) are out of scope.

### P5. Trust Markers Layer on Top of Ratings

Ratings are the floor, not the ceiling. Neighbourhood proximity, mutual connections, response consistency, and verified information are trust signals that differentiate providers beyond stars.

## Community Version Principles

_Parked — to be developed after Provider Version is validated._

### C1. Community Signals Over Vanity Metrics

Consistency, presence, and familiarity matter more than scores or rankings. No star ratings. No public leaderboards.

### C2. Familiarity Through Repeated Encounters

Trust is built over time through shared walks, repeated park visits, and recognisable patterns — not through a profile that claims trustworthiness.

### C3. Non-Commercial by Default

The Community Version is not a marketplace. Help, favours, and coordination happen between neighbours, not between customers and providers.

### C4. Invisible Infrastructure

The product should feel social, not transactional. Features that feel like "platform mechanics" (scores, levels, streaks) are a signal something has gone wrong.

---

## Revision Rule

Any proposal that changes or expands scope must answer:

1. Which version does this apply to?
2. What assumption does it test?
3. Why can't it wait?
4. What gets removed or deprioritised to make room?

If these cannot be answered clearly, the proposal is out.
