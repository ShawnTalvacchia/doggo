## Slide 2: The Problem
- Dogs need more than most owners can give them alone — more walks, more friends, more socialization, more exercise. Owners know this. They feel it every time they skip the evening walk or leave their dog home all day.
- The result: dogs that are under-stimulated and owners who carry quiet guilt about it.

**What exists today doesn't solve this:**

| Care Platforms (Rover, Care.com) | Social Groups (Facebook, Meetup) | Word of Mouth (Friends, neighbours) |
|---|---|---|
| Pure transactions between strangers. Trust is manufactured through reviews. No relationship before the booking. No community. No reason for your dog to get *more* care — just a way to outsource it when you have to. | Unstructured and noisy. No privacy controls. No path from "we walk at the same park" to "I trust you with my dog." Community exists but leads nowhere. | The best option — until favors pile up, expectations diverge, and the awkwardness of money poisons the friendship. Doesn't scale past 2–3 people. |

**Closing line:** The gap isn't just trust. It's that nothing today motivates owners to give their dogs a bigger, richer life — or makes it easy to do so.

---

## Slide 3: The Doggo Thesis
- A community model creates more people *providing* care and more people *using* care than any pure marketplace can. That's the thesis.
- Dogs are the most natural social connector in a city. Owners already meet at parks, recognize the same faces, build informal routines. Doggo gives that structure — and a reason to do more of it.

**The flywheel:**
1. **MEETS** — Show up at the park. Your dog makes friends. You make friends. Both of you get more exercise, more socialization, a better daily routine.
2. **TRUST** — Real relationships through repeated contact. You know the person, you know their dog. Not algorithms. Not reviews.
3. **CARE** — Book people you already know. Clear rates, no favors. Your dog gets more care because the barrier dropped — the trust was already there.

**Why this beats a marketplace:** On Rover, you're a stranger with a listing. On Doggo, you joined a park walk, connected with three regulars, and started picking up a second dog on your afternoon route. The path from "dog owner" to "earning money with dogs" is natural — and that path doesn't exist anywhere else. More providers, more affordable options, more care happening. Good for the dogs. Good for the owners.

**Closing line:** The community is the acquisition layer. The marketplace emerges from it — bigger and more trusted than any marketplace built from scratch.

## Slide 4: Three Ways In
- Groups are the connective tissue. Every user enters through one of three doors.

1. **Find Your Park** — Auto-generated groups for every major dog park. Open, no admin needed. Any member can post a walk. The lowest-barrier entry point — show up, see who's around, start building routine. Early groups are seeded with scheduled walks from community hosts: regulars who anchor the rhythm so new members always have something to join.
2. **Find Your People** — Neighborhood circles, breed groups, activity crews. Small, trusted, high-utility. Where mutual aid lives. (Small-Medium · Private · User-created)
3. **Find Your Help** — Provider-run groups with booking CTAs on meets. Your walker's schedule, your trainer's sessions. Community-wrapped service. (Medium · Open or Private · Provider-created)

## Slide 5: Trust is Earned, Not Claimed
- Every relationship progresses through four states. Users control the pace at every stage.

**Connection states:**
1. **NONE** — Default between strangers. See name, photo, dog, neighbourhood. No contact.
2. **FAMILIAR** — One-sided. You choose who gets expanded visibility. They can message-request you.
3. **PENDING** — Connect request sent. Awaiting acceptance. No messaging yet.
4. **CONNECTED** — Mutual. Full profile access. Direct messaging. Care CTAs visible if either offers services.

**Two user paths:**
- **The Cautious Path** — Locked profile. Attends meets. Selectively marks people as Familiar. Builds network at their own pace. May never go Open.
- **The Social Path** — Open from day one. Broadly discoverable. Receives message requests. Connects quickly. Both paths lead to trusted relationships.

## Slide 6: Privacy That Enables Community
- Every piece of content passes through two independent gates. If either gate fails, the content is hidden.

**Gate 1: Context** — Are you allowed to see content from this origin?
- Group member → See group content
- Meet attendee → See meet photos
- Open group → Anyone can browse
- Private group → Members only

**Gate 2: Relationship** — Does your connection with the author permit visibility?
- Connected → Full profile access
- Familiar (they marked you) → Expanded view
- Open profile → Anyone sees profile content
- Locked + None → Basic info only

**Rule:** Tags never expand audience. A tag is metadata for people who can already see the content — not a door to new viewers.

## Slide 7: The Key Insight
> Good fences make good neighbors. The booking system protects the friendship.

When neighbors become friends through their dogs, informal care follows. But informal favors are hard to keep balanced — and the awkwardness of raising that can poison the friendship.

| Without Doggo | With Doggo |
|---|---|
| "Should I offer money? Will they be offended? They watched my dog three times — I only watched theirs once. Do I owe them?" | "Sarah lives two streets over. She already knows my dog. Rates are posted. I book through the app. Nobody's keeping score." |

**Closing line:** "You trust each other more because the arrangement is clear, not less."

## Slide 8: User Journey — Tereza
**Archetype:** Routine Owner → Connector
**Provider dial:** Zero → barely turned

1. **Find a regular walk nearby** — Joins "Riegrovy Sady Dog Walks" park group. Shows up to a Thursday walk. Marks two attendees as Familiar.
2. **Form a smaller crew from regulars** — Creates a private group — "Vinohrady Evening Walkers" — invites five people she's met at the park.
3. **Help a neighbor when life happens** — The group grows to eight. Marek goes away — Tereza watches his dog. She becomes the default backup.
4. **Keep it fair without awkwardness** — Lists dog sitting with a modest rate. Not for income — because a posted rate makes expectations clear. The booking system protects the friendships.

**Closing line:** Park group → neighborhood group → casual care. The platform didn't push her to become a provider. The social dynamics did.

## Slide 9: User Journey — Daniel
**Archetype:** Occasional-Need → Regular
**Provider dial:** Zero (stays there)

1. **Find safe socialization for a reactive dog** — Park groups feel too chaotic. Finds "Prague Reactive Dog Support" — a private interest group. Requests to join.
2. **See what a meet looks like before committing** — Lurks for two weeks. Sees photos from a small, calm meet. Signs up. It goes well. Marks two people as Familiar.
3. **Build a support network around a shared challenge** — Attends bi-weekly meets. Connects with three members. Shares photos to the group (never his profile — Locked).
4. **Find professional help from someone he already trusts** — A Connected friend, Klára, is also a trainer. Joins her service group. Books a session. Trust was already built.

**Closing line:** Interest group → trust → care booking. He never used a park group. His entire experience was built around a specific need.

## Slide 10: User Journey — Klára
**Archetype:** Professional Provider
**Provider dial:** High

1. **Create a service channel that doesn't feel like advertising** — Creates an open service group with training meets — 4 spots, 350 Kč, booking CTA. Also joins a park group as a regular owner.
2. **Let organic connections drive client discovery** — Three park group regulars connect with her. They see her services on her profile. Two join her service group and book.
3. **Use shared content as social proof** — 15 members, two sessions a week. Members share photos from training. New visitors see the value and book.
4. **Scale the practice through the platform** — Adds dog walking, hires an assistant, creates a second service group. Groups are her primary client channel.

**Closing line:** Service groups as community-wrapped business. The booking CTAs are commerce — but the meets, photos, and relationships are real.

## Slide 11: How the Journeys Connect
- These aren't three separate products. They're three experiences of the same platform.

- Daniel books Klára's training sessions — Trust built in a shared interest group leads to a care booking
- Tereza's neighborhood group becomes a care network — Routine walks create the trust base. The booking system keeps it sustainable.
- Klára co-hosts events with Daniel's support group — Service groups and community groups cross-pollinate organically
- All three start from the same platform — Park groups are the common entry point. Different needs, same community infrastructure.

**Closing line:** One platform. Many paths. The community is the product.

## Slide 12: How Doggo Makes Money

| PRIMARY | SUPPLEMENTAL | FUTURE |
|---|---|---|
| **Care Transaction Fee** — 10–15% on bookings between connected users. Works because community built the trust. Clear records, cancellation handling, payment without the awkward. | **Business Profiles** — Freemium directory for local pet businesses — vets, groomers, pet shops. Community-embedded listings, not ads. Enhanced visibility for paid tier. | **Premium Features** — Advanced group tools, expanded gallery, priority visibility. Later and careful. Community features remain free — no paywalls on core social functionality. |

**Why on-platform?** Clear records. Cancellation handling. Care history. Payment without the social awkwardness.

## Slide 13: Why Now / Closing
- Your dog's neighbourhood crew.
- Meet at the park. Build trust over walks. Book care from people who already know your dog.

**Why now:**
- Urban loneliness is rising — but dog owners have a built-in icebreaker they use every day. The social infrastructure just doesn't exist yet.
- Remote and hybrid work means more flexible schedules, more midday walks, more availability to provide and use casual care.
- Pet humanization is accelerating — owners are spending more, expecting more, and thinking about their dog's social and emotional life in ways they didn't a decade ago.
- Prague specifically: a dense, walkable city with strong park culture, high dog ownership, and zero platforms connecting any of it.

**Stats:**
- 90,000+ dogs in Prague
- 42% of Czech households own a dog
- 0 unified community platforms
